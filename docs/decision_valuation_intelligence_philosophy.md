# Decision Valuation Intelligence Philosophy

Decision Valuation Intelligence translates analytical evidence into transparent decision tradeoffs. It does not optimize the organization, select an alternative, approve action, or execute work.

The governing unit is the comparison among alternatives. A single action is never valued in isolation. The baseline, current policy, do-nothing, defer, pilot, staged implementation, partial implementation, full implementation, expansion, contraction, abandonment, and switching alternatives remain explicit when relevant.

Evidence keeps its epistemic status throughout valuation. A randomized causal estimate, forecast, predictive model output, scenario assumption, expert judgment, imported finance input, LLM suggestion, missing input, and unsupported claim are not interchangeable. The valuation layer can combine them, but it must preserve where each claim came from and what it can legitimately support.

Expected value is not the whole decision. The framework also represents downside exposure, timing, reversibility, effort, capacity, implementation burden, strategic alignment, guardrails, information value, optionality, authority, and organizational coverage. A high-value alternative may still require escalation, and the baseline may be the rational recommendation.

Materiality connects evidence to action. Statistical or predictive evidence becomes decision-relevant only after it is translated through unit economics, scale, duration, implementation cost, opportunity cost, minimum meaningful benefit, and maximum acceptable harm.

Uncertainty is structurally different across alternatives. The same uncertainty may reduce the attractiveness of irreversible full commitment while increasing the attractiveness of a bounded pilot that limits downside and creates learning. The framework therefore avoids one universal uncertainty penalty.

Every valuation claim must be traceable:

```text
objective
-> strategy
-> tactic
-> lever
-> alternative
-> analytical evidence
-> financial translation
-> uncertainty
-> optionality
-> threshold
-> governed recommendation
```

The purpose of this layer is to make evidence-to-decision reasoning auditable. It is a deterministic foundation for future portfolio optimization, richer real-options analysis, and autonomous decision support, not those systems themselves.
